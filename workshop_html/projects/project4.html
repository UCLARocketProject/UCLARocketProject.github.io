Rocket tests sensing and ground sensing

Ways of making the world more comprehensible by transforming it into numbers only computers understand

Problem: We would only pretend to be engineers if we didn't, from time to time obviously, no need to be over-zealous, measure something

Measuring something turns out to be much more difficult than expected since the stupid nature refuses to be measured in the same units in all aspects.

What needs to be done when making measurements: Invariably, any measuring functional chain has to consist of:

Data -> Sensor -> Conversion -> Storing -> Viewing

As the electronics team we don't much care about Data and the Sensor. It is the problems of Conversion, Storing and Viewing that occupy us the most. Let's look at them closely.

Conversion: Sensors are usually stupid and by that I mean analogue and what's worst is it usually is not between 0-3.3V. Why between 0-3.3V?

0-3.3V is the simplified range in which our digital devices (such as the ATmega328 which is the processor for the most popular Arduinos) can convert data between analogue and digital, they use embedded ADCs. The problem is to get the signal from whatever it is outputted by the stupid sensor and make it vary nicely between 0-3.3V.

There are more problems than just whether the stupid (we're going to use stupid as a synonym to anologue) signal is between 0-3.3V. Some signals are the worst and come in a differential form meaning that we cannot be sure whether they vary from 0V to something, but we only know that they vary as a difference between their two lines. Now, we once thought that it would be easy enough to put one of those differential lines to ground and be done with it. Unfortunately that's not how it works1. What we need is a differential to single ended (anchord at ground at one point, e.g. 0-3.3V) signal driver.

Believe us, after reading through a dozens of websites, this is the one that finally had the answer. Paired with LTspice and we have our circuit! Of course, it actually adapting it is not that easy...

Oh, and by the way we have high precision rail-to-rail operational amplifiers, you can thank us later.

Storing: Now we have to store the data. If you're wondering about the step between converting the stupid/analogue signal to a readable range of 0-3.3V and getting the data into computer memory then don't worry. It literally takes 15 min, quite startling how with all these difficulty that part is that easy.

Fear no more, because our "computer memory" is about 100 bytes long and would run out in a second. So what do we do with all the data? We store it! That can take the form of storing it on an external flash memory (OK, an SD card) or communicating it to a device which has more than a 100 bytes of "computer memory".

Storing on an SD card requires a library for interfacing with the flash memory and has thankfully already been done. Take a look at the following Arduino library.

Communicating with another device will require the knowledge of communication protocols (e.g. UART, SPI) and will mostly be done by means of writing C/C++ code.

Viewing: And lastly viewing! We encourage you to take a look at the Visualization System as this will be work at some point connected with that, but for now viewing will include making sure the data is human-readable at least to some degree.

We are personally interested in viewing since we never thought C/C++ and artistic opportunity could be placed in the same sentence, but here we are, proven wrong by life itself.
